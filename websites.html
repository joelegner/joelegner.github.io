These are some online resources related to the purpose and goals of this website.

  • http://www.importanceofphilosophy.com. The author does not agree with everything on this site. There are errors in it. For example, it derides agnosticism by saying it is “an example of the evasion of the Law of Excluded Middle.” The Law of Excluded Middle comes from Aristotle and says, “A proposition either is true or is false, not neither and not both.” There is no middle ground. But another way to view agnosticism is that it is about reserving judgement. If we commit to the Law of Excluded Middle and apply it to the concept of god, we might rightly say, “By the Law of Excluded middle, it is true that either (1) god exists or (2) god does not exist. There is no third option.” Agnosticism does not deny this. But it adds, “However, I do not personally know the answer to whether god exists or does not exist.” The website seems to say that the fact that the idea “god” is floating around in the universe makes it mandatory that anyone considering things must fall on one side of the argument or the other or somehow their refusal to do so is an evasion–a word used in a derogatory manner. Nonsense. It’s perfectly reasonable to suspend disbelief in the absence of sufficient evidence. We can see this by applying it to something less devisive: the existence of intelligent alien life. The Law of Excluded Middle requires that either one of the two propositions is true: (1) there are intelligent aliens, or (2) there are no intelligent aliens. But nowhere in the The Law of Excluded Middle does it say thinking, rational creature must assent to one or the other position. The right thing to do when evidence is not convincing is to neither believe nor disbelieve. That’s agnosticism. There is still a lot of useful material on the site, but use it with caution.
