Use goals to enrich your life

“My fundamental goal is richness of life.” – Joe Legner

First, set goals. That gives you something to do. Even a goal like, “do nothing today” is a goal. Work toward them. Go on adventures. Enrich your life by stimulating yourself with new creative things to do.

Write your goals down in Apple Reminders. Use the year and quarter as your goals. Review those lists regularly, if not daily. This keeps the goals fresh in your mind. Check off those you complete. Enjoy that feeling! Celebrate!

Target 85% to 95% goal achievement. Anything lower, and you are probably not living life to the fullest. Anything higher, you are pushing yourself too hard. Stay in a sweet spot of sustainable effort, even pleasant effort, without burnout.

Remember when you are looking at your to-do list at the end of the day, “Perfection is overshooting the mark.” If you do everything on your to-do list, you are overshooting the mark. Relax a little.

When you set a goal, think immediately about the steps needed to achieve it. Make a plan. A plan is a series of events you think will result in the desired outcome.

“A goal without steps is merely a wish.”

Be realistic

Be realistic at all times. Do not fight nature. Avoid extreme judgments. Open your mind to nuance. Things are almost never – maybe always never – one thing. There is almost always – probably always – more than one way to look at things.

Be realistic when considering medication. Consider this observation learned by reading ADHD 2.0:

Some medications work in some people at some dose, some of the time.

Remember this as an example of being realistic.

Act, Feel, Imagine, Think

Remember you can only do these four things. They are not mutually exclusive. They are rather intertwined. But what else can you do? Nothing that I can think of.

  • Act: Do something with your body. The rest support this.
  • Feel: Experience emotions. Experience positive and negative valency of the emotions. Experience moods.
  • Imagine: Picture mental images in your head.
  • Think: Talk to yourself, either inwardly or outwardly.

Be optimistic

You might as well be optimistic. Remember not to feel entitled to things going well. You do not expect them to go well, but you aspire to it. Being optimistic probably has a positive effect on the probability of success.

Avoid perfectionism

You are not perfect. Nobody is. Avoid trying to be perfect. If you feel that you have to be perfect, the fact that you are assuredly not perfect will result in you being chronically disappointed in yourself. This is a problem. It leads to depression and feelings of inadequacy. It is not conducive to achieving your goals. Be imperfect. Be okay with being imperfect.

Remember you are still Joe fucking Legner. Be that guy. He is pretty cool. He is a good guy. Spend time practicing skills – drums, guitar, programming. Spend time learning. Spend time mediating.

Learn and practice communication skills

Use “I-messages”

Two-part I-message model:

“I feel feeling when event that caused the feeling.”

Try to word the event in neutral terms. Do not use it as a passive-aggressive way to dig at the other person. For example, “I feel angry because of what you’re saying about me” is better than, “I feel angry because you are trying to hurt me.”

When angry, pause

When angry, pause before communicating. Do not send an email while your blood is boiling. Step away. Take some time to cool off. Send it when cool, not hot.

“When angry, count 10 before you speak. If very angry, a hundred.” – Thomas Jefferson.

“If you speak when angry, you’ll make the best speech you’ll ever regret.” – Grocho Marx.

Remember not communicating is communication

You cannot help but communicate. Evading a communication still communicates something to the other person. This is an important insight. Non-communication is still communication. It is impossible not to communicate. Knowing this, it makes sense to choose to communicate. Then choose how and do it. See the results and gain experience from that cycle.

Use reason

Know what reason is. Know what its rules are. Use them to benefit you in your goal-seeking behavior.

The basis of reason is the idea that one claim follows from others. Deduction is reasoning from population to sample. It is deterministic Induction is reasoning from sample to population. It is probabilistic. Deduction is to reasoning as induction is to reasons.

Do not solely rely on reason. Richness of life demands more than cold reason. Reason seldom excites the emotion. Yet the human is an emotional creature. Be Kirk, not Spock.

Memorize these drum principles

STOP AT TENSION PRINCIPLE4 – Play at all times with relaxed muscles. Stop at the slightest feeling of tension. Control begins with muscular relaxation. Strive to stay completely relaxed from the neck, through the shoulders and arms, all the way down to the fingers

START WITH YOUR GRIP PRINCIPLE – The first thing you must do to be a drummer is grip the stick in the right way. See the 5 Basics video by Mike Michalkow.

PLAY FROM THE WRISTS PRINCIPLE - The wrists are the motor. Play from the wrists, not the elbows. You use less energy that way. Do as little work as possible. That way you will never tire out.

RUDIMENTS FIRST PRINCIPLE5 – Start each session with rudiments. Rudiments are the key to everything else. Without a solid handle on rudiments, the rest of your practice will be shaky. Rudiments are the bedrock upon which you build your foundations.

TWENTY TIMES PRINCIPLE6 – When practicing rudiments, practice 20 times without stopping.

METRONOME PRINCIPLE – A metronome keeps you honest. Practice with a metronome at different speeds, varying from extremely slowly to top speed, then back down to regular tempo again.

Warm up before practicing

This is a 7-minute warmup.

  1. Loosen your joints. Shake out your wrists for 15 to 30 seconds. Link your hands together and rotate your wrists, 10 rounds in each direction. Repeat these for 3 rounds.
  2. Stretch forearms. Hold up your right arm with your palm facing away from you. Using your left hand, pull your palm towards you, make sure your elbow is extended and hold for 5 seconds. You should feel a stretch along your lower forearm. Next, rotate your right hand down with the palm facing you. Again, pull your palm towards you, hold for 5 seconds. You should feel a stretch along your upper forearm. Repeat for your left hand. Then, repeat this exercise 3 times.
  3. Hand/fist stretches. With your hands in front of you, clench for fists tightly for 3 seconds. Then open your hands as widely as you can without moving your wrists and hold them there for 3 seconds. Repeat this exercise 3 times.
  4. Get a grip. Grip the drumstick at the first knuckle of the index finger at the spot on the stick where it bounces the most when dropping from horizontal.
  5. For the rest of the session, strive to stay completely relaxed from the neck, through the shoulders and arms, all the way down to the fingers.
  6. Play medium single-stroke roll for 1 minute. Start with sticks at 45 degrees to the drum. Pay special attention to the tips. Make sure they rise to the same line every time. Start slow, gradually increase tempo. At the 30-mark, start slowing down again.
  7. Play rebounding double strokes for 2 minutes. Check grip by dropping a stick to get only one extra bounce.
  8. Play single stroke roll to double stroke roll for 2 minutes.

Master single stroke rolls

Master your single stroke roll. Practice at various tempos between 60 bpm and 180 bpm with eighth notes, triplets, and sixteenth notes.

Master double stroke rolls

Double stroke rolls seem to be important. Mike Michalkow says he has to help the second beat at slow speeds, but at high speeds the rebound takes care of the second beat, making the two equal. Don’t go too slow. You have to go fast enough that the rebound works.

Work in 10-minute chunks

You want to practice things. You want to do productive things. You sometimes avoid and evade, because you dread the time, effort, and energy you will spend. By working things in defined, 10-minute bursts, you will lose that resistance. You will also be very productive. Event hough 10 minutes sounds short, it adds up if you do it daily.

10 MINUTES DAILY IS 60 HOURS YEARLY

Let our daily benchmark be 10 minutes per day. Assuming you do not skip more than 2% of the days in the year (6 days), this equates to 60 hours per year.

What about effort per workday instead?

First determine how many work days per year you actually go to work. At 5 days per week and 52 weeks per year, there are 260 potential work days per year. We have about 12 holidays which reduces the number to 248 days. My vacation is approximately 4 weeks or 20 days. The final number for planning is 228 days. Assuming again we do not miss more than 2% (4 days), we will do the activity 224 times in the year. Then 10 minutes per work day equates to 37 hours per year.

10 minutes per work day sums to 37 hours per calendar year.

It is now easy to scale these up and down to other times.

Use time-blocking

The calendar can be a powerful tool. Time-block tasks. This prevents other things from encroaching on them. It gives you the permission to set aside all other things during that time. Once complete you will likely feel a sense of accomplishment – a great feeling. You can forgive yourself for not continuing to work on the task after the timer goes off and you can stop.

Follow these steps to use time-blocking:

  1. Trigger: Deciding to commit to a task.
  2. Estimate the time and energy needed.
  3. Look for spots on your calendar where you expect to have both the time and energy.
  4. Add a calendar appointment.
  5. On the appointed day, at the appointed time, do the task.
  6. If the task is incomplete at the end, repeat the process – that is, if you are still committed to completing the job.
  7. If the task is complete, celebrate!

Make writing clear. That is the first priority. Know what your reader cares about. Give it to them as quickly as you can and then support it after that.

Read your writing out loud. If it does not sound good when read aloud, revise it. Edit it. Re-read it after that and repeat.

Use the principle of BLUF – bottom line up front. In an email, this means opening the email with the punchline. You might have one courtesy sentence, but then get to the point.

When you write an email, your first line should answer this question your reader will have: “Why did you write me an email (or a letter)?” You need to prove to the reader in the first one or two sentences what you offer them.

Only after BLUF do you explain your reasoning. Normally we build up from details through our logic and then reach our conclusion. We work from detailed to general. But when we write about that process, we follow the opposite path. We start with the conclusion, then we explain why we believe it, going from general to detailed.

Write from general to specific

What is the advice?

To write like Barbara Minto’s framework suggests, write from general to specific. Start with the most general thing you know about the subject. Make a statement about it – your primary claim. After that, support each claim with additional claims. You can go several levels deep this way, as needed.

Use the Minto Pyramid Principle

What is the advice?

When you write a paper of any kind, use this form:

  1. Each level is a summary of the level below.
  2. Each level contains the same kinds of things.
  3. Each level is logically ordered (induction, deduction, analogy, parts of a whole,metaphor, etc.)
