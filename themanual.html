  • 1/2: Tasks that take modest effort: laundry, doing dishes, reading for productively.

Use I-messages

Two-part I-message model:

“I feel feeling when event that caused the feeling.”

Try to word the event in neutral terms. Do not use it as a passive-aggressive way to dig at the other person. For example, “I feel angry because of what you’re saying about me” is better than, “I feel angry because you are trying to hurt me.”

Pause before communicating when angry

When angry, pause before communicating. Do not send an email while your blood is boiling. Step away. Take some time to cool off. Send it when cool, not hot.

“When angry, count 10 before you speak. If very angry, a hundred.” – Thomas Jefferson.

“If you speak when angry, you’ll make the best speech you’ll ever regret.” – Grocho Marx.

Remember not communicating is communication

You cannot help but communicate. Evading a communication still communicates something to the other person. This is an important insight. Non-communication is still communication. It is impossible not to communicate. Knowing this, it makes sense to choose to communicate. Then choose how and do it. See the results and gain experience from that cycle.

Understand metaphor

Metaphor is a way to understand something in one domain by leveraging your understanding of something in another domain.

Ground your concepts in physical metaphors. Remember metaphors like UP IS MORE and TIME IS AN OBJECT MOVING FROM FRONT TO BACK. Notice how these are used in the language. When other people use them, they understand them similar to the way you do. If they are from another culture, they might have different metaphors. Be prepared in case they do.

Metaphor is related to categorization. The two share a continuum.

Categorization < Continuum > Metaphor
“Love is an emotion.” “Love is a battlefield.”

How do we find meaning in metaphors?

Seeing, light, physical force (physics), sick person, war, hidden object, size (big, small), touching, containers, limbs, — in short all things we need to understand (grasp) to operate successfully in a physical world as a human being among other human beings: everyday life.

Use reason

Know what reason is. Know what its rules are. Use them to benefit you in your goal-seeking behavior.

The basis of reason is the idea that one claim follows from others. Deduction is reasoning from population to sample. It is deterministic Induction is reasoning from sample to population. It is probabilistic. Deduction is to reasoning as induction is to reasons.

Do not solely rely on reason. Richness of life demands more than cold reason. Reason seldom excites the emotion. Yet the human is an emotional creature. Be Kirk, not Spock.

Use cost-benefit model to decide

You make many decisions. Some are easy: to take a shower or not. Some are difficult: to go to college or not. A cost-benefit analysis is a way to make rational decisions that Use reason.

How do you do it?

When you decide between options, consider the costs and the benefits. Weigh the costs. Weigh the benefits. If the scale is not tipped in favor of benefits, select something else (if you can). Be honest about the tradeoffs.

When writing a proposal, benefit-cost ratio equals potential dollars received times probability of success divided by hours of proposal writing. Or, mathematically:

$$ \text{Benefit-cost ratio} = \frac{P(b) \times b}{h} $$

b = benefit, dollars
P(b) = probability of benefit (chance of proposal success) (0 − 1]
h = hours of effort to create the proposal

STOP AT TENSION PRINCIPLE7 – Play at all times with relaxed muscles. Stop at the slightest feeling of tension. Control begins with muscular relaxation. Strive to stay completely relaxed from the neck, through the shoulders and arms, all the way down to the fingers

START WITH YOUR GRIP PRINCIPLE – The first thing you must do to be a drummer is grip the stick in the right way. See the 5 Basics video by Mike Michalkow.

PLAY FROM THE WRISTS PRINCIPLE - The wrists are the motor. Play from the wrists, not the elbows. You use less energy that way. Do as little work as possible. That way you will never tire out.

RUDIMENTS FIRST PRINCIPLE8 – Start each session with rudiments. Rudiments are the key to everything else. Without a solid handle on rudiments, the rest of your practice will be shaky. Rudiments are the bedrock upon which you build your foundations.

TWENTY TIMES PRINCIPLE9 – When practicing rudiments, practice 20 times without stopping.

Warm up before practicing drums

This is a 5-minute warmup.

  1. Shake to loosen your joints. Shake out your wrists for 15 to 30 seconds. Link your hands together and rotate your wrists, 10 rounds in each direction. Repeat these for 3 rounds.
  2. Pull hands back – two ways. Hold up your right arm with your palm facing away from you. Using your left hand, pull your palm towards you, make sure your elbow is extended and hold for 5 seconds. You should feel a stretch along your lower forearm. Next, rotate your right hand down with the palm facing you. Again, pull your palm towards you, hold for 5 seconds. You should feel a stretch along your upper forearm. Repeat for your left hand. Then, repeat this exercise 3 times.
  3. Make fists and stretch out your hands. With your hands in front of you, clench for fists tightly for 3 seconds. Then open your hands as widely as you can without moving your wrists and hold them there for 3 seconds. Repeat this exercise 3 times.
  4. Grip the stick properly. Grip the drumstick at the first knuckle of the index finger at the spot on the stick where it bounces the most when dropping from horizontal.
  5. Strive to stay completely relaxed. For the rest of the session, strive to stay completely relaxed from the neck, through the shoulders and arms, all the way down to the fingers.
  6. Play single-stroke rolls. Play medium single-stroke roll for 1 minute. Start with sticks at 45 degrees to the drum. Pay special attention to the tips. Make sure they rise to the same line every time. Start slow, gradually increase tempo. At the 30-mark, start slowing down again.
  7. Play double-stroke rolls. Play rebounding double strokes for 2 minutes. Check grip by dropping a stick to get only one extra bounce.
  8. Play single-stroke to double-stroke rolls. Play single stroke roll to double stroke roll for 2 minutes. Alternate between LRLR.. eighth notes and LLRRLLRR... sixteenth notes.

Master single stroke rolls

Master your single stroke roll. Practice at various tempos between 60 bpm and 180 bpm with eighth notes, triplets, and sixteenth notes. Master double stroke rolls too.

Master double stroke rolls

Make the two beats equal. Help the second beat at slow speeds, but at high speeds let the rebound take care of the second beat. Don’t go too slow. You have to go fast enough that the rebound works. Master single stroke rolls too.

Work in 10-minute chunks

You want to practice things. You want to do productive things. You sometimes avoid and evade, because you dread the time, effort, and energy you will spend. By working things in defined, 10-minute bursts, you will lose that resistance. You will also be very productive. Event hough 10 minutes sounds short, it adds up if you do it daily.

10 MINUTES DAILY IS 60 HOURS YEARLY

Let our daily benchmark be 10 minutes per day. Assuming you do not skip more than 2% of the days in the year (6 days), this equates to 60 hours per year.

Assuming again we do not miss more than 2% (4 days), we will do the activity 224 times in the year. Then 10 minutes per work day equates to 37 hours per year.

Use time-blocking

The calendar can be a powerful tool. Time-block tasks. This prevents other things from encroaching on them. It gives you the permission to set aside all other things during that time. Once complete you will likely feel a sense of accomplishment – a great feeling. You can forgive yourself for not continuing to work on the task after the timer goes off and you can stop.

Follow these steps to use time-blocking:

  1. Trigger: Deciding to commit to a task.
  2. Estimate the time and energy needed.
  3. Look for spots on your calendar where you expect to have both the time and energy.
  4. Add a calendar appointment.
  5. On the appointed day, at the appointed time, do the task.
  6. If the task is incomplete at the end, repeat the process – that is, if you are still committed to completing the job.
  7. If the task is complete, celebrate!

Write like a pro

  • Write from general to specific.

Before you write, answer these questions:

  1. Who am I writing to? Who is my audience? What do they care about?
  2. Why am I writing to them? What outcome am I seeking? What is the goal?

Write to communicate effectively – that is to have the effects you want on your readers. Make writing clear. That is the first priority. Know what your reader cares about. Give it to them as quickly as you can and then support it after that.

Follow Bryan A. Garner’s steps:

  1. Brainstorm: Think up and write down points to make.
  2. Outline: Use the Minto Pyramid Principle to create an outline.
  3. Write: Write a draft as fast as you can without stopping. “Lock up the Judge and write in a headlong rush.”
  4. Wait: Set it aside for a few minutes or even days.
  5. Revise: Fix the overall structure. Move things around. Alternate with editing.
  6. Edit: Polish the language. Alternate with revising.

Read your writing out loud. If it does not sound good when read aloud, revise it. Edit it. Re-read it after that and repeat. Use short words and short sentences. Eliminate unnecessary words.

“A sentence should have no unnecessary words just as a machine should have no unnecessary parts.”

Use the principle of BLUF – bottom line up front. In an email, this means opening the email with the punchline. You might have one courtesy sentence, but then get to the point.

When you write an email, your first line should answer this question your reader will have: “Why did you write me an email (or a letter)?” You need to prove to the reader in the first one or two sentences what you offer them.

Only after BLUF do you explain your reasoning. Normally we build up from details through our logic and then reach our conclusion. We work from detailed to general. But when we write about that process, we follow the opposite path. We start with the conclusion, then we explain why we believe it, going from general to detailed.

Accept imperfection in your writing. Avoid perfectionism. As Wankat and Oreovicz (1993) say, “Manuscripts can be revised forever, and the reader will never think they are perfect.”
